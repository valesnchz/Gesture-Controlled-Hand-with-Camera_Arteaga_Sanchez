A Dual Approach for Optimized Hand Gesture Recognition
Using MediaPipe and AI Edge Deployment with ESP32-CAM
Sheyla Arteaga1and Valesska Sánchez1
1School of Biological Sciences and Engineering, Universidad de Investigacion de Tecnologia Experimental
Yachay, Urcuqui, Ecuador; sheyla.arteaga@yachaytech.edu.ec
Abstract 1
Hand gesture detection is an important approach to human-machine interaction, as it allows 2
users to communicate with electronic systems using visual commands. This article presents 3
a dual approach for optimized hand gesture recognition using MediaPipe and an AI-at-the- 4
edge (AI Edge) implementation. MediaPipe was used as a robust, computer-based method 5
for gesture recognition, while the AI-at-the-edge approach employed a lightweight FOMO 6
object detection model developed on Edge Impulse and implemented on the ESP32-CAM. 7
The AI model was trained to detect two classes of gestures: Close and Open, and its output 8
was connected to a servo-based physical response in a low-cost robotic hand prototype. 9
The system was evaluated using detection metrics such as completeness, accuracy, F1 score, 10
confidence score, and confusion matrix, as well as implementation-related metrics such as 11
inference time, memory usage, model size, and action validation. The proposed approach 12
shows how a computer-based gesture recognition method can be complemented by a 13
low-power hardware version for physical interaction using servomotors.14

1. Introduction 17
Hand gesture recognition has become an important tool in computer vision and 18
human-machine interaction (HMI) [ 1]. Controlling systems through visual commands 19
without physical contact is practical in robotics and assistive technology, where touching a 20
device is not always possible or convenient [ 2,5,8]. Most of these systems rely on powerful 21
computers or cloud servers to process images [ 1]. This reliance introduces latency, internet 22
dependency, and costs that make it challenging to create something portable or affordable. 23
Two approaches have emerged to solve this problem. The first keeps computation 24
on a host machine while using highly efficient landmark regression models. MediaPipe 25
Hands detects 21 three-dimensional hand keypoints in real time on a standard CPU. This 26
capability allows for continuous per-finger kinematic control without needing custom 27
training data [ 22,23]. This method has been applied to humanoid motion replication and 28
hybrid gesture recognition systems. It shows that pre-trained landmark models work 29
well across different users and conditions. The second approach moves inference entirely 30
onto the embedded device. AI Edge runs lightweight models directly on microcontrollers 31
next to the sensor, requiring no server or external connection [ 17]. The downside is that 32
microcontrollers have limited flash, RAM, and processing ability, which necessitates the 33
use of very compact architectures [ 3,4]. This work makes two concrete contributions. 34
First, we run a direct quantitative comparison between host-based landmark recognition 35

(MediaPipe) and on-device embedded inference (FOMO) under the same gesture set 36

2. State of the Art 40
2.1. Vision-Based Hand Gesture Recognition 41
Camera-based gesture recognition has received a lot of attention as a non-contact 42
way to interact with machines. Early studies used handcrafted features and traditional 43
computer vision techniques. The introduction of convolutional neural networks led to 44
major improvements in classification accuracy under different lighting and background 45
conditions [ 6,8]. Oudah et al. reviewed key computer vision methods for gesture recogni- 46
tion, discussing skin segmentation, contour analysis, and depth-based techniques. They 47
emphasized the practical benefits of camera systems compared to wearable options [ 8]. Eid 48
and Schwenker showed that CNNs trained on static hand images can effectively classify set 49
gestures. This study established a baseline for vision-only recognition without additional 50
sensors [6]. 51
Recent research has concentrated on real-time performance and reliability in realistic52
scenarios. Cui et al. reviewed deep vision methods for real-time gesture recognition, noting 53
that model design and input resolution are the main factors that impact both accuracy and 54
speed [ 2]. Gao et al. explored challenges in understanding gestures in real-world settings, 55
such as variations in hand orientation, visual similarities between different classes, and 56
the difficulty of distinguishing gesture areas from busy backgrounds [ 5]. Qi et al. linked 57
gesture recognition to physical control in human-robot interaction, presenting gesture 58
commands as a practical way to control robots [1]. 59

2.2. MediaPipe-Based Methods 60
MediaPipe is an open-source framework created by Google for developing real-time 61
perception pipelines on devices [ 25]. Its hand tracking module, MediaPipe Hands, features 62
a two-stage pipeline. First, it has a palm detector based on a lightweight SSD-MobileNet. 63
Second, it includes a landmark regression network that predicts 21 three-dimensional 64
keypoints for each hand in normalized image coordinates [ 26]. This design separates 65
detection from regression. As a result, the landmark model works on tightly cropped 66
hand areas, reducing the computational load of full-image inference. The model can run at 67
real-time frame rates on standard CPUs without needing GPU acceleration [26].68
Kim et al. used MediaPipe Pose to replicate humanoid robot motions. They found 69
that pre-trained landmark models work well for physical control tasks when geometric 70
relationships between keypoints are used to calculate joint angles [ 22]. Padhi and Das 71
suggested a hybrid system that combines DenseNet201 feature extraction with MediaPipe 72
hand tracking. They showed that landmark-based methods could integrate with custom 73
classifiers to expand gesture vocabulary beyond the built-in options [ 23]. Liao et al. merged 74
MediaPipe with a Transformer-based classifier for dynamic gesture recognition. Their work 75
demonstrated that the landmark stream can support sequence modeling for motion-based 76
gestures, not just static poses [24]. 77

2.3. AI Edge Deployment for Embedded Vision 81
Running inference on microcontrollers requires models that fit strict limits on flash 82
memory, RAM, and processing speed. Dovhal et al. explored object recognition approaches 83
for platforms with limited resources. They found that model quantization and simplifying 84
the architecture are the best methods for reducing memory usage without significant 85
accuracy loss [ 3]. Boyle et al. showcased real-time object detection on microcontroller 86
units using optimized lightweight architectures. Their results confirmed that detection is 87
possible on limited hardware when the model is designed for that purpose [4].88
Edge Impulse offers an effective development platform for such systems. It includes 89
data labeling, FOMO-based object detection training, int8 quantization through the EON 90
compiler, and direct export to embedded targets [ 7]. The FOMO architecture replaces full 91
bounding-box regression with a class heatmap for each grid cell. This change lowers both 92
computational complexity and memory needs, while still maintaining spatial awareness 93
within the frame. With a footprint of 81.3 KB of flash and 133.0 KB of peak RAM, the model 94
developed in this study fits within the constraints of the ESP32 [9,15].95
The reviewed studies show that both precise landmark tracking and compact on- 96
device detection are viable options, but they serve different use cases. This difference drives 97
the dual-mode architecture proposed in this work. 98

3. Materials and Methods 99
3.1. System Overview 100
The proposed system follows a dual approach for hand gesture recognition and 101
physical response. The first route uses MediaPipe as a robust computer-based method for 102
hand tracking and gesture interpretation. MediaPipe extracts hand landmarks from the 103
video stream, allowing the system to analyze different finger configurations such as open 104
palm, closed fist, index finger up, peace sign, OK sign, and other hand poses [ 20,25,26]. 105
The second route uses AI Edge with a FOMO object detection model developed in Edge 106
Impulse and deployed on the ESP32-CAM. This route was designed as a low-cost hardware 107
version with limited memory and processing resources, focused on the two gesture classes 108
Close and Open [3,4,7,9,11]. 109
In the MediaPipe-based route, the ESP32-CAM is used as the camera source, while 110
the computer processes the visual information and generates gesture or finger-state data. 111
This information is transmitted through WiFi to the ESP32-CAM-based controller. Then, 112
the ESP32-CAM sends control signals to the PCA9685 servo controller through I2C com- 113
munication, allowing the five SG90 servomotors to reproduce the detected gesture in the 114
robotic hand [12,13,21]. 115
Figure 1 shows the MediaPipe-based workflow. This route separates the recognition 116
stage from the physical actuation stage: the computer performs the landmark detection 117
and gesture interpretation, while the ESP32-CAM manages WiFi communication and servo 118
control. This division reduces the processing load on the ESP32-CAM and supports a more 119
fluid physical response. 120

3.2. MediaPipe-Based Computer Method 133
The method was implemented in Python using the MediaPipe Hand Landmarker 134
API [ 25,26] and OpenCV for frame capture and visualization. The script processes each 135
incoming frame from the ESP32-CAM stream, extracts the 21 hand keypoints, applies the 136
geometric finger-state computation, and maps the resulting binary states to individual 137
servo commands for each finger of the robotic hand. This translation from pixel coordinates 138
to physical finger position is the core contribution of this control mode.139

3.2.4. Gesture Classes and Dataset Acquisition 179
The MediaPipe-based control mode does not require a custom dataset it runs on 180
pre-trained weights that generalize across users and conditions without additional training 181
[25,26]. The dataset described here was collected exclusively to support the secondary 182
FOMO-based inference mode, which does require labeled images to train on.183
Two gesture classes were defined: open hand and closed fist. These two represent 184
the most visually distinct and biomechanically opposing postures of the robotic hand, 185
which made them the most practical choice for a compact embedded model [ 5]. A total of 186
316 images were captured directly with the ESP32-CAM [ 9,15], the same device used for 187
inference, so the model trains on images with the same resolution and optical characteristics 188
it will see at deployment. The open class has 161 images and the close class has 155. The 189
dataset was split into 253 images for training and 63 for testing, following an 80/20 ratio 190
[2]. 191
Capture conditions were varied deliberately different backgrounds, lighting setups, 192
hand orientations, and distances to reduce the risk of the model overfitting to a single 193
environment [5]. 194
3.3. AI Edge-Based Hardware Prototype 195
The physical prototype consisted of a robotic hand manufactured using 3D printing 196
with PLA polymer. The base structure was obtained from an open online robotic hand 197
design and adapted for the proposed gesture-controlled prototype [ 27]. This type of system 198
is related to human-robot interaction, where visual hand gestures can be translated into 199
physical actions [1]. 200
The printed structure included five independent fingers, allowing the prototype to 201
reproduce different hand configurations instead of moving as a single rigid structure. This 202
is important because each finger contributes to the final shape of a gesture, such as an open 203
hand, a closed fist, or other finger-based positions [ 20]. Therefore, the independent finger 204
design makes the prototype more flexible and closer to natural hand movement.205
The prototype was developed as a low-cost physical implementation for connecting 206
gesture recognition with robotic hand movement. Although the mechanical response is 207
limited by the precision of the actuation system and the printed structure, the prototype 208
provides a functional platform to demonstrate how recognized gestures can be converted 209

3.5. AI Edge Model Training 230
Once flashed onto the ESP32-CAM [ 9,15], the model runs continuously without any 231
external connection [ 17]. Each inference cycle starts when the OV2640 camera [ 11] captures 232
a frame, which is resized to 96 ×96 pixels and fed into the FOMO model [ 7]. The model 233
returns a detected class and a confidence score for each identified region [4].234
A confidence threshold of 0.60 was set to filter out uncertain detections. Only predic- 235
tions at or above this value trigger a command. Below that, the system holds its current 236
state and waits for the next frame. This threshold was chosen to reduce false activations 237
without making the system too slow to respond under normal conditions [16].238

When a valid detection occurs, the ESP32-CAM sends a command over I2C to the 239
PCA9685 servo controller [ 12,13], which drives five SG90 servomotors [ 21], one per finger. 240
For an open hand detection, all five servos move to their extended position, opening the 241
robotic hand fully. For a closed fist detection, all five servos retract, closing the hand 242
completely. 243
This mapping keeps the command logic simple and direct: one gesture, one state, five 244
servos moving together. There is no intermediate position and no partial response. The 245
physical output either matches the detected gesture or does not move at all [1].246
3.6. AI Edge Deployment and Action Logic 247
After training the FOMO-based object detection model in Edge Impulse, the model was 248
exported and integrated into the ESP32-CAM for local inference. This approach is suitable 249
https://doi.org/10.3390/1010000Version June 16, 2026 submitted toJournal Not Specified 12 of 20
for low-cost devices with limited memory and processing capacity, where lightweight 250
models are required [ 3,4]. During operation, the OV2640 camera captures an image frame, 251
which is resized to the configured input resolution of 96×96 pixels. The deployed model252
then detects the gesture region and assigns it to one of the two predefined classes: Close or 253
Open. 254
For each detection, the model provides a confidence score. If the confidence score is 255
greater than or equal to the selected threshold, the ESP32-CAM generates the corresponding 256
action signal. This signal is then sent to the servo motor driver, which controls the SG90 257
servomotors according to the detected gesture. In this way, the AI output is directly 258
connected to the physical actuation response of the robotic hand prototype.259
Initially, a confidence threshold of 0.60 was defined to reduce false servo activations 260
caused by uncertain predictions. If the detected gesture does not reach this threshold, the 261
system does not execute a new movement and the previous servo position is maintained. 262
This condition helps avoid unstable movements when the prediction confidence is low or 263
when the hand is not clearly detected. 264

3.7. Evaluation Metrics 265
The proposed system was evaluated using two groups of metrics: FOMO-based object 266
detection metrics and AI Edge deployment metrics. Object detection metrics were used to 267
analyze whether the model could correctly detect and classify the hand gestures as Close 268
or Open, following common evaluation practices for machine learning and vision-based 269
recognition systems [ 2,6,16]. Deployment metrics were used to verify whether the trained 270
model could operate locally on the ESP32-CAM and generate a physical response through 271
the servo controller and SG90 servomotors, considering the memory, latency, and resource 272
constraints of embedded AI systems [3,4,17–19]. 273
As summarized in Table 8, the object detection evaluation included precision, recall, 274

validation. Together, these metrics allowed the evaluation of both the detection perfor- 277
mance of the FOMO model and the feasibility of its integration into the ESP32-CAM-based 278

when the hand moved too fast relative to the frame rate of the stream [5].294

The physical response of the system was validated by observing servo behavior during 322
live gesture detection. When the model produced confidence scores well above the 0.60 323
threshold, both gestures triggered the correct servo response: open hand extended all five 324

In cases where the confidence score landed close to the 0.60 threshold, the system 328
occasionally skipped the command and held its current state. This behavior is expected: 329
the threshold exists precisely to avoid acting on uncertain predictions. However, it also 330
means the system can feel unresponsive when the gesture is not cleanly positioned or lit 331
[16]. Adjusting the threshold lower would reduce missed activations but would likely 332
increase false triggers. 333
The results show the main idea of this work: MediaPipe and AI Edge can work 334
together for hand gesture recognition and robotic hand control [ 1,25]. The MediaPipe 335
route was used as the main control method because it tracks the hand landmarks on the 336
computer in real time [ 20,26] and sends finger-state information to the robotic hand. Since 337
the computer performs the recognition process [ 22,24], the ESP32-CAM only receives the 338
control data and moves the servomotors [15]. This made the physical response smoother.339
On the other hand, the FOMO-based AI Edge route was used to test whether gesture 340
detection could be implemented directly on a low-cost device with limited memory and 341
processing power [ 3,4]. In this case, the model achieved an overall F1 score of 83.8% on 342
the validation set. For the backgroundless classes, the accuracy was 0.88, the completeness 343
was 0.80, and the F1 score was 0.84 [ 16]. These results show that the model performed 344
acceptably for a low-cost hardware version [ 9,10], although the most significant difference 345
in this route is that the response was slower than the MediaPipe route.346
To check how the model generalizes outside the training environment, we tested it 347
on a new subject. As shown in Figures ??and ??, gesture recognition worked correctly in 348
real time—confidence scores were 0.91 for the Open class and 0.98 for Close. These results 349
align with the quantized model’s offline metrics: Close reached 78.6% accuracy (21.4% of 350
samples were misclassified as background) while Open hit 81.5% (with 18.5% background 351
confusion), for an overall F1 of 83.8%. 352
This behavior may be related to the image conditions used during detection. For 353
example, changes in lighting, background, hand distance, and hand orientation can hinder 354
gesture detection [ 2,5]. Furthermore, the input resolution used for the FOMO model was 355
https://doi.org/10.3390/1010000Version June 16, 2026 submitted toJournal Not Specified 18 of 20
limited [ 7], resulting in a reduction in visual detail [ 11]. Therefore, some images of hands 356
may have appeared similar to the background, especially when the hand was not centered 357
or was not clearly visible. 358
Furthermore, these results also explain the behavior observed in the physical prototype. 359
The FOMO model recorded an inference time of 981 ms on the ESP32-CAM [ 17,18]. This 360
value indicates that the model can run on the device [ 19], but it does not respond instantly. 361
During testing, this delay became noticeable in the movement of the servomotors [ 12,13]. 362
In some cases, the user had to repeat or slightly adjust the hand gesture until the system 363
detected it accurately enough and activated the movement.364
The confidence threshold was important to keep the robotic hand movement stable. A 365
threshold of 0.60 was selected to avoid activating the servomotors with uncertain predic- 366
tions. When the confidence score was above this value, the system executed the detected 367
movement. When the confidence score was below the threshold, the previous position was 368
maintained. This prevented incorrect servo activations [ 14] and reduced unstable changes 369
between the open and closed positions. 370
Overall, the results show that each approach contributed differently to the system. The 371
MediaPipe approach provided better performance for smooth, continuous handheld robotic 372
control, as the recognition process was performed on the computer [ 23,26]. This allowed 373
the ESP32-CAM to focus primarily on receiving control data and moving the servo motors 374
[15]. In contrast, the AI Edge approach was somewhat slower, but it demonstrated that 375
gesture detection can be implemented directly on a low-cost device like the ESP32-CAM 376
[3,4]. Therefore, both approaches are useful in different ways: MediaPipe offers a more 377
robust option for real-time control [ 1,25], while FOMO on the ESP32-CAM represents a 378
lighter, hardware-centric alternative [7,17]. 379
While the results were positive, the tests also revealed some limitations that should be 380
considered for future improvements. One of the main limitations was the response time 381
of the AI Edge approach. The FOMO model reported an inference time of 981 ms on the 382
ESP32-CAM [ 4], resulting in a noticeable delay between the hand gesture and the servo 383
movement. This delay did not prevent the prototype from functioning, but it did affect the 384
immediacy and naturalness of the robotic hand’s response during testing.385
Another limitation was the small number of gesture classes used in the AI Edge model. 386
The FOMO model was trained with only two gestures: Open and Close. This facilitated the 387
training and implementation of the model on the ESP32-CAM [ 7], but it also limited the 388
number of actions the robotic hand could perform. In its current version, the prototype 389
can only reproduce basic opening and closing movements [ 21], so it does not yet represent 390
more complex hand gestures, which was a noteworthy feature for MediaPipe [26].391
The variability of the dataset also posed a limitation. The model’s performance can be 392
affected by lighting conditions, the background, the distance from the hand, its orientation, 393
and its position relative to the camera [ 2,5]. If the hand is not clearly visible, the model may 394
classify the gesture as background or generate a confidence score close to the threshold 395
[16]. This can cause the system to maintain the previous position instead of executing a 396
new movement. 397
Future work should focus on improving both the gesture detection model and the 398
robotic hand’s physical response. For the AI approach, the dataset should be expanded 399
with more images from different users, lighting conditions, backgrounds, distances, and 400
hand orientations [ 2,8]. This would help the model detect gestures more reliably and reduce 401
the number of cases classified as background. 402
Future versions should also include more gesture classes, not just Open and Close [ 1]. 403
For example, the system could be trained to recognize gestures such as pointing with the 404
index finger, the peace sign, a thumbs-up, or individual finger movements [ 20,26]. This 405
https://doi.org/10.3390/1010000Version June 16, 2026 submitted toJournal Not Specified 19 of 20
would make the robotic hand more useful, as it could reproduce a wider variety of hand 406
commands and positions. 407
Furthermore, future work could test different input resolutions, confidence thresholds, 408
and model optimization strategies to reduce inference time on the ESP32-CAM [ 4,18]. 409
Reducing inference time would make the servo motor’s response faster and more natural.410
The servo motor’s control logic could also be improved by adding smoother transitions 411
between positions instead of directly switching from one state to another.412
Finally, future work should include a more formal comparison between the MediaPipe 413
route and the AI Edge route. This comparison could measure latency, response time, 414
detection stability, and servo motor movement accuracy under the same test conditions 415
[1,3]. This would provide a clearer assessment of when it is better to use a computer-based 416
approach and when it is better to use a low-cost AI Edge implementation [17].417
5. Conclusions 418
This work presented a dual approach for hand gesture recognition and robotic hand 419
control using MediaPipe and AI Edge. The MediaPipe route provided a smoother and more 420
stable physical response because the recognition process was performed on the computer,421
allowing the ESP32-CAM to focus on communication and servo control.422
The FOMO-based AI Edge route showed that gesture detection can run directly on a 423
low-cost device such as the ESP32-CAM. The model achieved an overall F1 score of 83.8% 424
for the Open and Close gesture classes. However, the inference time of 981 ms caused a 425
noticeable delay in the robotic hand response. 426
The prototype successfully connected visual gesture recognition with physical move- 427
ment using an ESP32-CAM, a PCA9685 controller, and five SG90 servomotors. Although 428
the system was functional, its performance was limited by response time, dataset variability, 429
and the reduced number of gesture classes. 430
In summary, the proposed system demonstrates that a robust, computer-based ap- 431
proach and a low-cost AI implementation at the network edge can work together within the 432
same gesture-controlled robotic hand framework. This confirms the potential of combining 433
gesture recognition with affordable, hardware-based actuation.434
